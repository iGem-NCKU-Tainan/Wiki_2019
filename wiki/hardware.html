Hardware

In Taiwan, National Health Insurance doesn’t cover blood p-Cresol inspection fee. In addition, each examination is expensive, cost about 1500NTD (About 50 USD). To make p-Cresol examination more cost-effective and easy, our team designed a detector to achieve this goal with the help of synthetic biology.

Device Overview

Our device consists of two parts. In the first part, we pretreat whole blood to extract plasma for GFP reaction. In the second part, the presence of p-Cresol in plasma will induce p-cresol sensing E. coli to produce fluorescent protein which can be detected by photoresistor. From the GFP intensity, we will be able to indicate the p-Cresol concentration.

Blood Separation

There are white blood cells in human’s blood which could be lethal for E. coli. In order to make our p-Cresol sensing E. coli able to survive in blood sample and produce fluorescent protein, filtering out blood cells beforehand is necessary. We try two ways to achieve this goal while integrating all the steps we need. One is constructing a lab-on-chip centrifugal microfluidic system, the other one is using paper-based sensor.

A lab-on-chip centrifugal microfluidic system

In microfluidic centrifugal platform, fluidic processing steps such as the mixing of reagents or the moving of samples can be automated simply by implementing different spinning profiles. Also, It can be easily operated by health professional’s in diagnostic centers. With the advantages mentioned above, we’re able to extract blood plasma and mix it with p-Cresol sensing E. coli easily and rapidly.

For the centrifugal platform, we choose a brushless motor instead of DC motor to reduce operational and mechanical noise, also to obtain higher torque to weight ratio. In addition, we combined three layers of acrylic as a chip holder where we can put our microfluidic chips.

In the beginning, we used Polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) to make microfluidic chips. We created acrylic molds to cast PDMS into desired shape, then we bond another layer of pdms onto it to cover the channels with the help of oxygen plasma treatment. From June to July, we made three different versions with minor changes solving the problems we faced.

After we made the mold for version 3, we consulted with professor JUI-CHEN YANG. He suggested us to use slide glass instead of another layer of PDMS to cover the channel, which the bonding will be stronger compared to PDMS-PDMS bonding. So we tried but we haven’t tested it yet.

In addition to PDMS microfluidic chip, we made sculpted acrylic disc to replace microfluidic chip and chip holder. By doing so, we can eliminate the bonding problem and also decrease the loading on the motor.
