Design

Overview

In order to achieve our goals, we designed our E. coli Nissle to have two functions.
The first function is to eliminate the p-Cresol producing bacteria, specifically Clostridiumi strains, which we have done so by equipping our E. coli Nissle with a high specificity bacteriocin. The second one is to degrade the precursor of p-Cresol, tyrosine by diverting the original pathway.

For biosafety reasons, we implement biocontainment by deletion one of our two genes candidates which are yadF gene and dapA gene.

In an effort to prevent the accumulation of more uremic toxins due to the ingestion of our engineered E. coli Nissle inside the human body, we knocked out the tnaA gene

During our research, we found out that Pseudomonas has the ability to sense p-Cresol. So we designed a p-Cresol (-GFP) reporter system by cloning Pseudomonas's p-Cresol sensing promoter to E. coli and culture it in our device for detection.

Secretion tag (yebF)

yebF is a well-known E. coli secretory protein with unknown function. With a size of only 12kDa, previous iGEM teams[1] and other researchers[2] have used it as a secretary tag for recombinant proteins and proven functional. So, we decided to fuse it with our bacteriocin to enable its secretion from our E. coli Nissle.

Bacteriocin

Previous researchp[3] reported that a bacteriocin-like gene was found in Clostridium butyricum MIYAIRI 588 (CBM588), a bacterium used in probiotics and was proven to have a bactericidal activity to certain strains of C. difficile. The bacteriocin (later called CBM-B) self-cleaves itself into two parts, but only the C-terminal region possesses the bactericidal activity.[3]

Tyrosine transporter (tyrP)[4]

It’s the gene that is involved in transporting tyrosine across the cytoplasmic membrane, E.coli Nissle in our case.
Hence, we coded it from original E.coli MG1655 and designed a new biobrick which incorporated into our E.coli, allowing it to take in more tyrosine than Clostridium. By doing so, our E.coli can provide competition to Clostridium and reduce the absorption of additional tyrosine by Clostridium, which in turn reduces the amount of p-Cresol it produces.
