Design

In order to achieve our goals, we designed our E. coli Nissle to have two functions. The first function is to eliminate the p-Cresol producing bacteria, specifically Clostridium strains, which we have done so by equipping our E. coli Nissle with a high specificity bacteriocin. The second one is to convert the precursor of p-Cresol, tyrosine by diverting the original pathway.

For biosafety reasons, we implement biocontainment by deletion one of our two genes candidates which are yadF gene and dapA gene.

In an effort to prevent the accumulation of more uremic toxins due to the ingestion of our engineered E. coli Nissle inside the human body, we knocked out the tnaA gene.

During our research, we found out that Pseudomonas has the ability to sense p-Cresol. So we designed a p-Cresol (-GFP) reporter system by cloning Pseudomonas's p-Cresol sensing promoter to E. coli and culture it in our device for detection.

How can we reduce the population of the main uremic toxin producer?

Secretion tag (yebF)

yebF is a well-known E. coli secretory protein with unknown function. With a size of only 12kDa, previous iGEM teams[1] and other researchers[2] have used it as a secretary tag for recombinant proteins and proven functional. So, we decided to fuse it with our bacteriocin to enable its secretion from our E. coli Nissle.

Bacteriocin

Previous research[3] reported that a bacteriocin-like gene was found in Clostridium butyricum MIYAIRI 588 (CBM588), a bacterium used in probiotics and was proven to have a bactericidal activity to certain strains of C. difficile. The bacteriocin (later called CBM-B) self-cleaves itself into two parts, but only the C-terminal region possesses the bactericidal activity.[3]

We designed three constructs, one full-length CBM-B fused with yebF, two constructs of C-terminal domain of CBM-B and yebF with two different linkers - a GS linker (three Glycine Serine repeat: GSGSGS) and a TB linker (thrombin cleavage site: LVPRGS).

How can we convert tyrosine into useful substances?

Tyrosine transporter (tyrP)[4]

It’s the gene that is involved in transporting tyrosine across the cytoplasmic membrane, E. coli Nissle in our case.

Hence, we coded it from original E. coli MG1655 and designed a new biobrick which incorporated into our E. coli, allowing it to take in more tyrosine than Clostridium. By doing so, our E. coli can provide competition to Clostridium and reduce the absorption of additional tyrosine by Clostridium, which in turn reduces the amount of p-Cresol it produces.

FNR promoter

The FNR (Fumarate and Nitrate reductase Regulatory) protein of E. coli is an oxygen – responsive transcriptional regulator required for the switch from aerobic to anaerobic metabolism. Under anoxic conditions, the FNR protein binds to DNA target sites and controls the expression of the corresponding genes[5].

Previous iGEM team[6] used an artificial FNR promoter with two tandem FNR binding site, which has been reported to have a higher protein expression (1.8x) level under anaerobic condition[7].

We are concerned that TAL expression level may affect E. coli cell growth in normal condition because it converts amino acid into other non-nutrient compounds. Also, the target protein expression site is in our large intestine, where oxygen levels are low. So in the TAL construct, we decided to use the FNR promoter to prevent cell stress in normal condition.

This is also the part that we are working on for part characterization. We will repeat the experiment from the 2013 TU-Eindhoven.

Tyrosine ammonia-lyase (sam8)

Tyrosine ammonia-lyase (TAL) is an enzyme that converts tyrosine into p-Coumaric acid. We will engineer E. coli Nissle to express both tyrP and TAL, to turn the excess tyrosine inside the gut to a substance useful for humans - p-Coumaric acid[8]. By reducing the total amount of p-Cresol precursor, tyrosine, we can thus reduce the amount of p-Cresol being produced by other gut flora.

According to a research article that ranked different species of TAL[9], TAL from Saccharothrix espanaensis has proven to have the highest conversion rate for tyrosine, and luckily, this enzyme has already been submitted as an iGEM biobrick Part:BBa_I742146. But the iGEM biobrick contains a native ribosome binding site (RBS) from the original bacteria. We decide to change the native RBS into a strong RBS in iGEM collection B0034 to improve expression in E. coli Nissle.

How do we test it?

In 2013, iGEM Uppsala has developed a method to measure TAL function[10]. By using nanodrop UV-Vis spectrum and n-octanol extraction, we can extract p-Coumaric acid from the growth medium of bacteria and use its absorbance profile to quantify it. We also performed Western Blot to detect the enzyme and verify the biobrick improvement.

Avoid some negative impacts on CKD patients

TnaA gene knocked out[11,12]

A lot of bacteria have the ability to fermentate amino acids and one of those bacteria is Escherichia coli. Escherichia coli possesses the enzyme tryptophanase (tnaA) that catalyzes the fermentation of tryptophan to indole, which is a type of uremic toxins. To prevent the production of indole, we knocked out the tnaA gene.

Biosafety

yadF gene knocked out

The yadF gene of Escherichia coli encodes a β-class carbonic anhydrase (CA), an enzyme that assists rapid interconversion of CO2 and water into carbonic acid, protons and bicarbonate ions. E. coli requires a constant supply of bicarbonate as a metabolic substrate during normal growth, however the low concentration of CO2 in the air and its rapid diffusion from the cell means that bicarbonate needs to be made spontaneously in vivo to meet needs.[13] Subsequently, if this gene is knocked out, E. coli is unable to turn the CO2 into bicarbonate fast enough before the CO2 diffuses out and cause the cell to die.

Thereby, we decided to use the yadF gene as a kill switch. Inside the gut where we want the E. coli to be able to survive, there is a high enough concentration of CO2 in the atmosphere to diffuse into the cell continuously, giving the cell a chance to convert the CO2 into the much-needed bicarbonate ions. However, when the E.coli exits the human body and into the air, the low concentration of CO2 diffusing into the cell will result in death.

We tested our knockout strain by streaking it out on Cm plate and placing it in different conditions, one in a normal incubator at 37℃ to simulate the normal CO2 concentration of the atmosphere, and one in a 5% CO2 incubator at 37℃ to simulate the CO2 concentration in the gut.

dapA gene knocked out[14,15]

dapA (4-hydroxy-tetrahydrodipicolinate synthase) is an enzyme necessary for cell wall synthesis. By deleting this gene, the bacteria will have to depend on exogenous diaminopimelate (DAP) for cell wall synthesis and growth, making it a kill switch as the bacteria will die without suppliance of DAP.

To test whether E. coli can live with dapA condition and vice versa, we made LB plate contained DAP and knocked out dapA in E. coli Nissle additionally E. coli Mg1655 as our control. We tested through genotype check with colony PCR and phenotype check by comparing two plates: dapA plate and normal LB plate.

p-Cresol sensing system[16,17,18]

For our device, we decided to make a sensor for sensing p-Cresol. According to our research, we have discovered a group of bacteria known as Pseudomonas can sense p-Cresol naturally. In a previous study, the capability of Pseudomonas to sense p-Cresol is commonly used in the detection of p-Cresol in the environment.

Our construct is primarily centred on Pseudomonas fluorescens (PC24), because it has a gene cluster that consists of a positive inducible operon, pchR, which allows it to sense p-Cresol and promote the transcription of the downstream region to degrade the p-Cresol. pchR is the gene that translates into activator proteins that can bind with the inducer (p-Cresol) and leads to the conformation of the protein. As a result, the activator protein binds with p-Cresol and the eventual protein-p-Cresol complex will then bind onto the p-Cresol sensing region that is placed just downstream of pchR and start transcription of the gene downstream. We replaced the downstream region from the original degrading function of the operon with GFP, so that instead of degrading the p-Cresol it will light up. Finally, the operon is then placed into a plasmid and transformed into E. coli DH5a.

To put it succinctly, we engineered an E. coli that can sense p-Cresol and illuminate under the induction of p-Cresol. Therefore, we can use this E. coli to create a biosensor to sense p-Cresol.
